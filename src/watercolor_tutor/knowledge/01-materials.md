# Materials & Setup

## Paper
Use **140 lb (300 gsm) cold-press** watercolor paper. The weight matters: anything
lighter than 140 lb buckles and warps badly once wet, and the puddles pool in the
warped dips instead of lying flat. Cold-press has a slight tooth (texture) that grabs
pigment evenly — good for beginners. Avoid printer paper and sketch paper entirely;
they pill, tear, and repel water. A 9x12 inch block or pad is a sensible starting size.

## Brush
A single **round brush, size 8**, is enough to start. A size 8 round holds a useful
reservoir of water while still coming to a fine point, so one brush covers both broad
washes and thin lines. Synthetic-sable blends are inexpensive and hold water well.
Skip cheap stiff craft brushes — they splay and won't hold a bead of water.

## The two-jar water system
Keep **two jars of water**: one "dirty" jar for rinsing pigment off the brush, and one
"clean" jar reserved only for mixing new color. Mixing with clean water keeps your
colors bright; rinsing in a separate jar stops muddy water from dulling every mix. Change
the dirty jar when it turns grey.

## Securing the paper
Tape the paper down to a rigid board on all four edges with **artist's tape** (low-tack
masking tape). This holds the sheet flat as it expands when wet, and leaves a clean white
border when removed. Painter's tape works; cheap office tape tears the paper surface.

## Palette
A **limited 6-tube palette** is plenty: a warm and cool version of each primary — e.g.
warm + cool red, warm + cool yellow, warm + cool blue. Six tubes can mix almost any color
a beginner needs, and a limited palette keeps mixes from going muddy.

## Do / Don't
- Do tape paper to a board before starting.
- Do keep two separate water jars.
- Don't use printer or sketch paper.
- Don't start with a huge brush set — one good round 8 beats ten bad brushes.
