# Brush Control

## Grip and control
Hold the brush **further back on the handle, around the midpoint**, not choked up near
the ferrule like a pencil. Holding further back relaxes your strokes and lets the whole
arm move, giving smoother lines; gripping near the bristles produces tight, cramped,
shaky strokes. Keep the wrist loose.

## Line width by pressure
Width comes from **pressure, not brush size**. The *tip* of a round brush makes a thin
line; pressing down onto the *belly* of the brush spreads the bristles for a thick
stroke. Practice a single stroke that goes thin → thick → thin by pressing down in the
middle and lifting at the ends — this "pressure swell" is the foundation of every
expressive line.

## Loaded vs thirsty brush
Know the two brush states:
- A **loaded brush** is full of watery paint and carries a visible bead of liquid at the
  tip — use it to lay color and washes.
- A **thirsty brush** is one you've dipped and then blotted on a towel until just damp —
  use it to *lift* excess water, soften an edge, or pick up a bead. Same brush, two jobs.

## Controlling the bead
When you load the brush, a small drop ("the bead") forms at the tip. Touch the bead to a
towel briefly to control how much paint releases. Too much paint floods the stroke; a
blotted brush gives a controlled, even line.

## Practice drills
- **Parallel lines**: paint 10 evenly spaced horizontal lines, keeping width constant —
  trains steady pressure.
- **Pressure swells**: the thin-thick-thin stroke, 6 times in a row.
- **Dry-brush drag**: a thirsty brush dragged fast leaves a broken, textured line — useful
  later for grass and texture.

## Do / Don't
- Do hold the brush back near the middle of the handle.
- Do vary pressure, not brush, to change width.
- Don't choke up near the bristles (causes shaky lines).
- Don't overload — blot the bead first for control.
