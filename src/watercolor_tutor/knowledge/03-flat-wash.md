# The Flat Wash

## Board angle
Tilt your board to about **15 degrees** (prop it on a book). The slight angle makes
gravity pull a small reservoir of paint — "the bead" — to the bottom of each stroke, so
you can carry that bead down the paper and keep the wash even. A flat board lets paint
pool randomly; too steep an angle makes it run.

## Mixing the wash
For a pale flat wash, mix roughly **1 part pigment to 3 parts water (1:3)** — it should
look like weak tea. Critically, **mix more than you think you need.** Running out of
mixture partway down a wash is the #1 cause of banding (visible horizontal stripes),
because the second batch never matches the first. Mix the full puddle before the first
stroke.

## The stroke sequence
1. Load the brush fully and lay a horizontal stroke across the top.
2. A bead of paint gathers at the bottom edge of the stroke (thanks to the 15° tilt).
3. Lay the next stroke just below, **overlapping that wet bead** — this pulls the color
   down smoothly with no line between strokes.
4. Continue top to bottom, always catching the previous bead.
5. **Finish by lifting the final bead** with a thirsty (blotted) brush so it doesn't run
   back up and create a backrun.

## Drying
Lay the paper **flat** to dry once the wash is laid. Drying on a tilt lets pigment
migrate downhill and dry unevenly (darker at the bottom).

## Wash faults
- **Streaks / banding**: usually the paint was too dry, the brush underloaded, or you
  worked too slowly and the edge dried before the next stroke caught it. Fix: more water,
  fuller brush, work at a steady pace.
- **Backruns ("cauliflowers" / "blooms")**: a wetter drop runs back into a damp area,
  pushing pigment into a feathery blotch. Caused by too much water or disturbing a
  half-dry wash. Fix: don't go back into a drying wash; lift the final bead.

## Do / Don't
- Do tilt the board ~15° and mix extra paint.
- Do overlap the wet bead, working top to bottom.
- Don't let the wash dry on a tilt.
- Don't go back into a half-dry wash (causes backruns).
